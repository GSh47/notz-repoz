To prove that the set {P0​(x),P1​(x),…,Pn​(x)} is linearly independent in the vector space Pn​(F) (the space of polynomials of degree at most n over a field F), we need to show that the only solution to the equation:

c0​P0​(x)+c1​P1​(x)+⋯+cn​Pn​(x)=0

is c0​=c1​=⋯=cn​=0, where c0​,c1​,…,cn​ are scalars in F.

Step 1: Write out the polynomials Pk​(x)

For k=0,1,2,…,n, the polynomial Pk​(x) is defined as:

Pk​(x)=xk+xk+1+⋯+xk.

However, this definition seems redundant because xk+xk+1+⋯+xk simplifies to xk (since all terms are the same). This suggests there might be a typo in the problem statement. Instead, let’s assume the correct definition is:

Pk​(x)=xk+xk+1+⋯+xn,

which is a polynomial of degree n for each k. This interpretation makes sense in the context of the problem.

Step 2: Write the linear combination

Consider the linear combination:

c0​P0​(x)+c1​P1​(x)+⋯+cn​Pn​(x)=0.

Substitute the definition of Pk​(x):

c0​(x0+x1+⋯+xn)+c1​(x1+x2+⋯+xn)+⋯+cn​(xn)=0.

Step 3: Collect like terms

Rewrite the equation by collecting terms with the same power of x:

c0​x0+(c0​+c1​)x1+(c0​+c1​+c2​)x2+⋯+(c0​+c1​+⋯+cn​)xn=0.

For this equation to hold for all x, the coefficient of each power of x must be zero. This gives us the system of equations:

⎩⎨⎧​c0​=0,c0​+c1​=0,c0​+c1​+c2​=0,⋮c0​+c1​+⋯+cn​=0.​

Step 4: Solve the system of equations

From the first equation, c0​=0. Substituting c0​=0 into the second equation gives c1​=0. Substituting c0​=c1​=0 into the third equation gives c2​=0. Continuing this process, we find:

c0​=c1​=c2​=⋯=cn​=0.

Step 5: Conclude linear independence

Since the only solution to the equation c0​P0​(x)+c1​P1​(x)+⋯+cn​Pn​(x)=0 is c0​=c1​=⋯=cn​=0, the set {P0​(x),P1​(x),…,Pn​(x)} is linearly independent in Pn​(F).

Replacement theorem

Let V be a vector space that is generated by a set G containing exactly n vectors, and let L be a linearly independent subset of V containing exactly m vectors. Then m<n and there exists a subset H of G containing exactly n−m vectors such that L∪H generates V.
